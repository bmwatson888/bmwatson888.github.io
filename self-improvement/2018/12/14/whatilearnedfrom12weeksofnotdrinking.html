    6 Things I Learned From Not Drinking For 12 Weeks

    6 minute read , Posted: December 14, 2018

    I like to look at life’s challenges as opportunities. I’ve had a fungal infection on my small toenail on my right foot for years, and this year I made it one of my goals to take care of it. The issue really had no impact on my life, other than a thick, yellow toenail on my smallest toe. I had genetically inherited the proclivity to catch this kind of nail fungus from my dad, who had major problems with nail fungus all his life. Frankly, I’m lucky I don’t have bigger issues with it.

    Anyway, the doctor’s recommendation for treatment was to begin a regiment of anti-fungal medication, that I would need to take daily for 12 weeks. The downside? This medication is very hard on the liver, so I would not be able to drink for the entire window of treatment. I surprised myself to find I was kind of excited about the prospect, the excuse not to drink.

    Prior to this, I wouldn’t have called my drinking habits heavy, but I wouldn’t have called them healthy either. I’d probably estimate 5-10 drinks per week, on average. Now that I put a number on it, it does seem like a lot. I’d typically have at least one drink a night, and more on the weekends. At least one weekend night, I’d probably have 3-5 drinks. Twelve weeks was certainly the longest period I’ve gone without drinking in the last 20-ish years (since I was 21).

    So what did I learn over these 12 weeks? Let’s find out:

    1) It was harder than I thought. Drinking is ingrained in our society and some friendships are even based on it. It certainly was a big part of my life, particularly on the weekends. Quite a few of our group activities were literally “to drink”. We’d go wine tasting, bar hopping, or visit breweries. Of course, socializing with friends was the real activity here, but there are healthier ways to accomplish that. I went on a work trip during this period, and during evening get-togethers, attendees would ask me what was wrong with me like I was a terminally ill patient, or shove drinks in my face. Unfortunately, with drinking off the table, time with friends certainly diminished, and when we did get together, my not drinking was a common topic of conversation, as we made plans for the future for my eventual return back to the drinking-world.

    On the habitual side, the first few weeks were difficult, but not oppressive. There were times in the evenings when I’d really crave a beer, or during board game nights when I’d normally always be drinking. For some reason, probably because I’m a masochist, I made sure to challenge myself by leaving the fridge fully stocked with beer (I wouldn’t recommend this for other people though). After 3 weeks or so, the habit went away, and the urge for the evening drink went away. I did still crave a drink (and still do) during board game nights, but I just replaced beer with kombucha or flavored sparkling water, and I was fine. My advice for ending a bad habit would be to replace it with a good habit.

    2) Drinking has its place. Although I eventually came to the conclusion that drinking had too big a place in my life, there certainly is still a place for it. Drinking, on occasion, can strengthen bonds between friends in ways that cannot be done sober. Although drinking is a stress in itself, it can help to relieve stresses in other areas of your life, by allowing you to forget your cares for the night. The danger here, is the addictive nature of constantly needing to forget your cares. If you find you need to constantly drink to be happy, alcohol isn’t for you. You need to seek help, or make other significant changes in your life.

    3) My mood was much more stable. After the first few weeks, my mood stabilized quite a bit. The constant highs and lows were gone, and I felt a much more consistent feeling of contentment. I’m not really sure why, but I attribute this to my body chemistry stabilizing from no longer processing alcohol, keeping better hydration, and getting better sleep. Speaking of which…

    4) Sleep has noticeably improved. I immediately found I was able to sleep through the night easier, and feel more refreshed when waking. Anything more than two drinks, typically meant I would wake in the middle of the night and have trouble going back to sleep. Those days are essentially gone. If you have sleep issues, cut out alcohol and see what happens. Even if you don’t have sleep issues, experiment with a couple weeks without alcohol and see if you notice a difference.

    5) Overall I don’t feel all that different. Other than what I’ve previously mentioned, I don’t really feel all that significantly different, both physically and psychologically. I still have my days when I feel great, and occasional days when I feel ‘meh’. In comparison, I feel significantly better after a week of fasting, so for my body, diet is more important than whether or not I drink. When hanging out with friends, I still had fun and never felt like I needed to drink to participate socially. The thought then occurred to me, if I don’t feel that different, why do it?

    6) Drinking is expensive. Doing a little math, assuming a ball-park of $3 per drink, with an average of eight drinks per week, comes out to a conservatively low number of $100 per month. I bet I spent way more a month than that, since quite often we’d be drinking $8 brewery beers. This one speaks for itself, I gave myself a significant income boost by not drinking.

    So, now that I can drink again, what are my habits like today? It’s been over a month since I’ve completed my medication regiment, and I’ve made some interesting observations. First, I no longer enjoy the taste of beer (as much). I used to love the hoppy IPAs, but now they no longer seem appetizing. However, my tastes have increased significantly for red wine, particularly cabernet. I’m not really sure how to explain this change.

    Second, I don’t really enjoy drinking anymore. In social settings, I really got used to having all my wits about me, having a clear mind, and having full control of my physical faculties. After a few drinks, I lose these characteristics, and I take less enjoyment from interactions…as if I’m limited in some way (which I am). Turns out, I like being clear headed. I’m very pleased with this result, as I had feared my return to drinking might result in worse habits than before.

    Finally, I do still drink on occasion, but the amount has significantly reduced. I almost never have more than two drinks on a single day, and that’s once a week at most. If I have 3-4 drinks in a given week, that’s a lot of drinks for me. The daily urge to drink I had previously no longer exists. If you’re concerned about your drinking habits, maybe a break will work for you too!

    Oh, one final note. Over the twelve week period, I lost around 10 pounds; however, I did couple this experiment with a calorie reduction, and also switched my diet to Ketogenic, so I can’t firmly conclude ‘not drinking’ was a factor in weight loss. Common sense though, would suggest that it can’t hurt!
